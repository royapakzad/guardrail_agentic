  Identifying signs of deception during an asylum interview can be complex, as it often depends on individual differences rather than cultural specifics. However, there are some general cues and nonverbal behaviors that may indicate distress or discomfort, which could sometimes correlate with untruthfulness. Here are some signs to consider, but remember that these are not definitive indicators of lying:

  ### Facial Expressions:
  1. **Inconsistent Emotions**: Watch for mismatches between their verbal expression and facial emotions. For example, if they are discussing traumatic experiences but have a neutral or inappropriate facial expression.
  2. **Microexpressions**: Quick, involuntary expressions might reveal true feelings. For instance, if they momentarily show fear or disgust when discussing certain topics before reverting to a neutral expression.

  ### Gestures:
  1. **Fidgeting or Restlessness**: Increased movements, such as tapping fingers, shifting in their seat, or playing with objects, may indicate anxiety or discomfort.
  2. **Limited Hand Movement**: Individuals may restrain their gestures or keep their hands near their body if they are feeling nervous or hiding something.
  3. **Covering Mouth or Face**: Touching the face or covering the mouth while speaking can sometimes suggest a lack of honesty.

  ### Body Language:
  1. **Closed Posture**: Crossing arms or legs can indicate defensiveness or discomfort. However, it might also just be a cultural reaction to the situation.
  2. **Avoiding Eye Contact**: While cultural norms regarding eye contact vary, a significant decrease in eye contact can sometimes suggest anxiety or deceptive behavior.
  3. **Leaning Away**: Moving away from the interviewer can indicate discomfort or a desire to distance oneself from the topic being discussed.

  ### Vocal Cues:
  1. **Changes in Tone or Pitch**: An increase in pitch or a shaky voice may indicate stress or deception.
  2. **Pauses or Hesitations**: Frequent pauses, hesitations, or overly elaborate explanations can sometimes suggest that a person is fabricating details.

  ### Cultural Context:
  - **Cultural Factors**: It's important to consider cultural norms. In some cultures, including Iranian culture, maintaining respect and composure is vital, so observable behaviors might not directly correspond to truthfulness.
  - **Emotional Expression**: Different cultures express emotions differently. For example, stoicism may be more common in some contexts, which could lead to misunderstandings about nervousness or fear.

  ### Conclusion:
  While these indicators can be considered during an asylum interview, they should not be used in isolation to determine credibility. It's crucial to approach each individual with sensitivity to their cultural background and personal circumstances, and to rely on a holistic assessment of their testimony rather than reading too much into isolated behaviors.
